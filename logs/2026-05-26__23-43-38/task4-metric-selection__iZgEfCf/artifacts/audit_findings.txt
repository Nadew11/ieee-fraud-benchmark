AUDIT FINDINGS REPORT: EVALUATION OF FRAUD DETECTION MODEL
Date: May 27, 2026
Auditor: Lead AI & Financial Analyst

We have performed a comprehensive audit of the draft model performance evaluation prepared by the junior analyst. Our review identified several critical analytical errors, misleading claims, and missing operational checks. This report details our findings, explains why they matter, and corrects the metrics.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FINDING 1: Inclusion of Automated Test / QA Data (Batch 99) in Performance Metrics
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Claim in Draft: "The XGBoost fraud detection model was evaluated against 5,650 held-out transactions... Accuracy: 88.3%, AUC-ROC: 0.9182."
- Correction & Fact: The raw predictions export contains 150 records from Batch 99 (TransactionID prefix '9000') with an artificial ~50% fraud rate (73 positive, 77 negative) where the model achieved exactly 100% accuracy and an AUC-ROC of 1.0000. 
- Why it matters: Batch 99 represents QA integration testing or synthetic scripts with a deterministic structure, not real production transactions. Including this batch heavily inflates the reported AUC-ROC from 0.8861 (clean data) to 0.9182. This gives the false impression that the model is significantly more accurate at distinguishing fraud than it actually is, leading to overconfidence in production performance.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FINDING 2: Inclusion of Out-of-Period Data
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Claim in Draft: "Scoring period: January 15 - April 30, 2026" (implied in the methodology as using all 5,650 rows).
- Correction & Fact: The dataset contains 585 records with a 'scored_at' date after April 30, 2026 (ranging from May 1 to May 11). This includes 500 real transactions (prefix '300x') and 85 QA transactions (Batch 99). These records fall completely outside the specified quarterly holdout window.
- Why it matters: To maintain rigorous quarterly business reviews, evaluation windows must be strictly bounded. The inclusion of post-period records represents a pipeline export error that distorts the actual quarterly statistics and violates the defined scope.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FINDING 3: Mathematical & Logical Blunder on Performance Targets
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Claim in Draft: "Based on the strong AUC of 0.9182 and accuracy of 88.3%, this model meets our performance targets... Targets (AUC > 0.80, Accuracy > 95%)"
- Correction & Fact: The analyst claimed the model met all performance targets, but the model's reported accuracy of 88.3% is far below the stated deployment target of 95%.
- Why it matters: This is an egregious logical error in a business-critical deployment document. It demonstrates a lack of basic verification that could have easily been flagged during a superficial review.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FINDING 4: Inappropriate Use of Accuracy for Highly Imbalanced Fraud Data
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Claim in Draft: Using "Accuracy: 88.3%" and a target of "Accuracy > 95%" as primary evaluation metrics.
- Correction & Fact: In a realistic fraud dataset where the clean fraud prevalence is only 3.24% (162 out of 5,000), a naive baseline model that classifies all transactions as non-fraud (predicts 0 for everything) would achieve an accuracy of 96.76%. This naive model easily beats the 95% accuracy target but catches $0 in fraud and is completely useless.
- Why it matters: Evaluating a fraud model on overall accuracy is highly misleading. It penalizes model alerts (which create false positives but are necessary to catch true fraud). The team must instead prioritize AUC-ROC, Precision, Recall, and F-1 Score to assess discriminative power and operational efficiency.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FINDING 5: Complete Neglect of Operational Capacity and Financial Impact
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Claim in Draft: "Applied threshold of 0.5... is recommended for production deployment. No further tuning is required."
- Correction & Fact: The draft completely ignores the operational capacity of the fraud review team (1,500 alerts/day) and the financial consequences of the chosen threshold. At the 0.5 threshold, the clean model would generate 667 alerts over 5,000 transactions. Projected to the quarterly volume of 2,800,000, this creates 373,520 alerts, or ~4,150 alerts per day (using 90 days per quarter). This is 2.7x higher than the team's capacity of 1,500 alerts/day, which would completely overwhelm operations and cause thousands of alerts to go unreviewed, leaking millions in fraud.
- Why it matters: Models cannot be deployed in a vacuum. A threshold of 0.5 is operationally infeasible and financially suboptimal. The threshold must be tuned specifically to align with review capacity and minimize total quarterly business cost.
